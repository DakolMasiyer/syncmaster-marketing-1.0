### IG-M3-EDU-02

Month 1 you learned what sync is.
Month 2 you learned what sync pays.

Month 3 question: what separates a catalogue that gets briefs from one that doesn't? ↓

— — —

DEPTH OVER WIDTH

A supervisor doesn't need 400 tracks.
They need 40 tracks that are deeply documented, consistently available, instantly deliverable.

Depth means:
→ Every track has a complete metadata record
→ Every track has stems prepared and labelled
→ Every co-writer credit is documented and signed
→ Rights are confirmed on every piece before any brief arrives

Width means: you have a lot of stuff.
Supervisors can't use "a lot of stuff."

— — —

MOODBOARD YOUR CATALOGUE

Great catalogue managers don't think in track titles.
They think in placement types.

Build a mood index:
— Upbeat / celebratory (sports, celebration, product launch)
— Tension / build (thriller, drama, documentary)
— Intimate / emotive (healthcare, charity, human interest)
— Epic / cinematic (films, streaming originals, trailers)
— Background / atmospheric (hospitality, retail, ambient brand)

Every track should have a primary and secondary placement type.
Supervisors search this way. Speak their language.

— — —

VERSIONING IS A COMPETITIVE ADVANTAGE

One track. Multiple submissions.
Standard: full mix
Instrumental: vocals removed
60-second edit: advertising format
30-second edit: social media / shorter spots
Extended: episodic or documentary use

Most composers submit one version.
The composers getting repeat briefs submit five.

— — —

THE TECHNICAL FLOOR

No brief gets fulfilled without these:
→ WAV masters at 24-bit / 48kHz minimum
→ ISRC registered
→ Stems labelled by instrument group (not by track number)
→ BPM and key confirmed
→ Mood and instrumentation tags completed

This isn't advanced practice.
It's the baseline.

WHAT THE NUMBERS SHOW

A single confirmed placement generates:
→ Upfront sync fee: $800–$3,000 for regional / short-term (typical entry range)
→ Master fee (if you control your master): adds 30–50% to the upfront total
→ Performance royalties: ongoing for 1–3 years per placement, through your PRO

Three placements in 90 days, conservatively:
Upfront income: $4,500–$7,400 across three deals
Performance royalties: accumulating for years from each placement date

Compare that to streaming:
1 million streams = roughly $3,000–$4,000 at standard rates.
Getting to 1 million streams for most independent composers: 6–18 months.

Sync wins on timeline — if you can get the placements.

— — —

WHAT MAKES IT VIABLE AS PRIMARY INCOME

Critical variables:
→ Catalogue size: 30–60 sync-ready tracks (not 300 rough tracks)
→ Placement rate: one per quarter is sustainable; one per month is scalable
→ PRO registration: every placement must be registered to collect performance income
→ Repeat brief flow: supervisors who return = predictable pipeline

— — —

THE HONEST LIMITS

Sync is not passive income.
Each placement requires documentation, brief response, negotiation, and delivery.

It's also not consistent month-to-month.
A quiet month with no briefs is followed by a month with four.

What sync offers that streaming doesn't:
— Significantly higher per-use income
— Performance royalty compounding over years
— Industry relationships that generate further work

What streaming offers that sync doesn't:
— Volume (thousands of micro-transactions vs. dozens of large ones)
— Speed (release today, earn this month)
— No gatekeeping (no vetting required)

— — —

THE HONEST ANSWER

Yes — with 3+ confirmed placements per year, PRO registration, and a documented catalogue of 40+ tracks, sync can be primary income.

No — if you haven't completed rights documentation, don't have stems, or haven't registered with a PRO.

The talent is there. The infrastructure has to catch up first.
