### IG-EDU-03

Two licences. One placement. Most composers only know about one.

If you've ever wondered why sync deals feel complicated — every placement requires two separate legal permissions. ↓

🔑 THE SYNC LICENCE
Permission to pair your composition with visual content.
Controlled by: the publisher (or you, if you self-publish).

🔑 THE MASTER LICENCE
Permission to use the specific recording.
Controlled by: whoever owns the master — often the label, unless you recorded independently.

One placement = two separate negotiations.

If a label owns your master:
→ They share in every sync deal you ever make
→ A supervisor has to negotiate with two parties
→ That friction kills deals

"One-stop" means you control both.

Supervisors prioritise one-stop tracks.
Fewer calls. Faster deals. No surprises.

### THREAD-04

1/ The music supervisor didn't reject your track because it wasn't good.

They rejected it because it wasn't clear.

Here's what "rights clearance" actually means — and why it's the most important thing you can fix this week. 🧵

2/ A "cleared" track means:
→ The composition is original (no uncleared samples)
→ The rights owner(s) can be identified immediately
→ A licence can be granted without involving a third party
→ The master is owned by someone who can sign off today

If any of those aren't true, the track isn't cleared. Full stop.

3/ The sample problem.

If your track contains a sample — even a small one, even flipped — and it's not cleared, the track is legally unusable for sync.

A production company cannot broadcast a track with uncleared samples. The liability is theirs.

They will skip your track, no matter how good it is.

4/ The split rights problem.

Your label owns the master. You own publishing.

Now a supervisor needs two clearances. Two sets of negotiations. Two timelines.

Most supervisors move on. They find a one-stop track instead.

5/ The registration problem.

You can't collect performance royalties from a placement if you're not registered with a PRO (SAMRO, COSON, SOCAN, PRS, ASCAP, etc.).

And if your tracks don't have ISRCs, you can't be found in their databases.

Every placement has royalties attached. But you have to be registered to receive them.

6/ The fix for most of this:
→ Stop using uncleared samples (or clear the ones you have)
→ Retain your master rights before signing deals
→ Register every track with a PRO
→ Get ISRCs from a distributor or your national music body

This is months of work. Start now.

7/ SyncMaster verifies all of this before we pitch a single track.

### STANDALONE-IG-02

Stems close deals.

Music supervisors will often shortlist your track — then ask for stems before confirming the placement.

Stems = individual instrument tracks from your session exported separately.

Why they need them:
→ To fit your music to picture (trimming, looping, adjusting levels)
→ To isolate or remove elements that clash with dialogue
→ To create custom edits for different cut lengths

If you can't deliver stems, you can lose a placement you'd already won.

The fix: keep every project file. Export stems at the end of every session. Store them somewhere accessible.

20 minutes when you finish a track.
It can save — or win — a placement months later.

### IG-M2-EDU-08

Your track got shortlisted.

The supervisor likes it. The picture edit is locked.

Then they ask: "Can I get stems?"

What happens in the next 2 hours determines whether you get the deal.

Here's what professional stems look like. ↓

WHAT STEMS ACTUALLY ARE:
Separate audio exports of each main element in your track.
Not a "no drums" version. Not a "no music" version.
Individual layers that can be mixed independently.

THE STANDARD STEM CATEGORIES:
→ Full mix (the complete, mastered track)
→ Drums and percussion (isolated)
→ Bass (isolated)
→ Harmony / chords (keys, guitar, pads)
→ Melody / lead (primary melodic element)
→ Atmosphere / FX (pads, risers, ambience)
→ Vocals (if applicable — main and backing separate)

THE TECHNICAL SPEC:
→ Format: 24-bit WAV, 48kHz (broadcast standard)
→ Length: same as full mix, to the exact sample
→ Peak level: –6dBFS maximum
→ Naming convention: Artist_Title_Stem-Name.wav

THE DELIVERY STANDARD:
→ Packaged in a single named folder
→ Shared via Google Drive or Dropbox (link ready to send)
→ Delivered in under 2 hours. Ideally under 30 minutes.

When a director wants to drop out percussion for a quiet scene, they need your drums stem.
When an editor needs to extend a section, they need your loops.
If you can't deliver stems cleanly and fast, another composer gets the placement.

Build your stem library as you produce.
Not after the brief arrives.
