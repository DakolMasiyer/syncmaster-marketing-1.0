### TWEET 2/7

2/ Let's be clear: this isn't a streaming problem.

Afrobeats artists are all over the charts. Global reach, massive streams.

But sync licensing — where the real upfront money is — operates through a completely different system. Charts don't open those doors.

---
### TWEET 3/7

3/ Music supervisors work from approved libraries and direct referrals.

They can't use a track until:
→ Rights are confirmed
→ Files meet spec
→ Composer responds within 24 hours

Most African composers aren't in those libraries. When they are, the process breaks down at rights.

---
### TWEET 4/7

4/ The rights issue is structural, not personal.

Label deals in Africa often leave composers without clear master ownership. Sample clearances are incomplete. Publishing is split or unregistered.

A supervisor finds a great track and hits a wall.

---
### TWEET 5/7

5/ The metadata issue is invisible until it matters.

No BPM tag. No mood categorisation. No ISRC.

Without metadata, your track can't be searched in any licensing database. You're invisible even when you're technically "in the room."

---
### TWEET 6/7

6/ This isn't a talent problem.

African music is globally beloved. The demand is real. The money is moving.

It's moving to whichever composers have their infrastructure right.
