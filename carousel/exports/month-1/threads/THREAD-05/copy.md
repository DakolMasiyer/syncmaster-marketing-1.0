**Platform:** Twitter/X | **Pillar:** Culture | **Persona:** Both | **Date:** 2026-06-15

---
### TWEET 1/7

1/ Afrobeats isn't a trend.

It's a reference point for an entire generation of global productions.

Here's what that means for the composers making it — and why most of them aren't benefiting financially. 🧵

---
### TWEET 2/7

2/ Let's establish context.

Afrobeats, Amapiano, Afro-soul and Afrofusion are showing up in:
→ Hollywood film trailers
→ International luxury brand campaigns
→ K-drama and anime soundtracks
→ European fashion week content
→ Global streaming drama scores

The sound is everywhere.

---
### TWEET 3/7  ⚠ 338 chars (over 280 limit)

3/ But "everywhere in culture" doesn't mean "everywhere in licensing."

When a production uses African music, they typically license through:
→ A major distributor's catalogue
→ A Western sync library holding the rights
→ A blanket deal that doesn't trace back to the original composer

The composer may never know the placement happened.

---
### TWEET 4/7

4/ This isn't a conspiracy. It's an infrastructure problem.

Music supervisors can't use what they can't find, can't clear, or can't confirm.

African composers producing world-class music are often invisible in the systems supervisors search.

---
### TWEET 5/7

5/ What's changing:

A growing number of African composers are taking rights management seriously.

Self-publishing. Retaining masters. Getting ISRCs. Registering with PROs.

The infrastructure is starting to catch up with the output.

---
### TWEET 6/7

6/ And the demand is accelerating.

Supervisors are actively looking for authentic African music — not African-influenced. African.

The appetite is real. The supply is real. The bridge is what's missing.
