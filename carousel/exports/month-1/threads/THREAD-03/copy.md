# THREAD-03 — What a supervisor looks for in submissions
**Platform:** Twitter/X | **Pillar:** Education | **Persona:** Tunde | **Date:** 2026-06-08

---
### TWEET 1/7

1/ A music supervisor doesn't listen to your track the way you do.

They're not looking for emotion. They're solving a brief.

Here's what they actually check — in order. 🧵

---
### TWEET 2/7  ⚠ 287 chars (over 280 limit)

2/ Before they press play:
→ Is this one-stop? (Can I clear both licences from one person?)
→ Are there uncleared samples?
→ Do I have a WAV file? (MP3 is a non-starter)
→ Is there proper metadata? (BPM, key, mood, ISRC)

If any of those are "no" — the track is out. They never heard it.

---
### TWEET 3/7

3/ If they do press play, they're listening for:
→ Does it match the brief tempo and mood?
→ Does it loop or transition cleanly?
→ Are there sections that work for picture edits?
→ Is the mix broadcast-spec clean?

Not: "Is this a great song?" That question doesn't matter here.

---
### TWEET 4/7

4/ After they like it:
→ Can I get stems?
→ What's the response time? (Does this composer reply fast?)
→ What's the rate?
→ Is this person professional to work with?

This is where "great music" stops mattering and "great to work with" starts.

---
### TWEET 5/7

5/ Most African composers get filtered out at step 1.

Not because of talent. Because of process:
→ Rights not confirmed
→ No WAV file ready
→ No metadata
→ No one to respond quickly

---
### TWEET 6/7

6/ Fix those four things and you're ahead of 80% of composers pitching to supervisors right now.

The music you've already made? That's the hardest part.

The process? Learnable. Fixable. Buildable.
