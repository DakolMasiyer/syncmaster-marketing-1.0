# Africa\

## Hook

There's a reason the biggest ad campaigns of the last three years sound the way they do.

## Body

They're drawing from an African sonic vocabulary — the polyrhythm, the tonal density, the emotional directness — that's become the global reference point for music that feels both fresh and deeply human.

Burna Boy. Tems. Wizkid. Asake. Black Sherif.

And dozens of composers you've never heard of, whose music has scored productions you've definitely seen.

Here's the thing: the sound has arrived globally. The infrastructure hasn't followed.

Sync licensing — the process by which music is licensed for film, TV, and advertising — is still largely inaccessible to African composers.

Not because the music isn't good enough.

Because the system wasn't built with them in mind:
→ Catalogue tools that don't account for different rights structures
→ No relationships between African PROs and international sync libraries
→ Supervisor networks that don't reach Lagos, Nairobi, Accra, Johannesburg
→ No infrastructure for discovering, vetting, and connecting African composers at scale

Africa's sonic identity is shaping global culture. The composers creating it deserve to be compensated for that influence.
