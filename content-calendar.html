IG-EDU-03 · Pillar: Education | Persona: Tunde | Topic: Master vs. sync licence
Two licences. One placement. Most composers only know about one. When a production company wants to use your music, they need to clear two rights: The sync licence — permission to synchronise your composition with their visual content. This covers the underlying song: the melody, the chords, the lyrics. The master licence — permission to use your specific recording of that song. If you wrote the song AND recorded it yourself, you control both. You're a one-stop. Supervisors love this. It means one conversation, one deal, one clean clearance. If you used a co-writer or your label owns the masters, it gets complicated fast. The supervisor has to chase multiple parties. Most won't bother. They'll move to the next track. This is why so many talented African composers get skipped over — not because their music isn't good, but because the rights situation is unclear. Before you submit anywhere, know the answer to this: who owns your master? Who owns your publishing? If the answer is "I'm not sure" — fix that first. SyncMaster only places composers with clear documentation. It's not a barrier — it's a competitive advantage. CTA: DM us "RIGHTS" and we'll send you a free checklist.

IG-EDU-08 · Pillar: Education | Persona: Tunde | Topic: ISRC codes explained
Your track has no ISRC code? That's the first thing we fix. An ISRC — International Standard Recording Code — is the unique identifier assigned to your specific recording. Think of it as a barcode for your music in the global rights system. Every track needs one. Here's why: → Rights organisations use it to track performances and pay royalties → Streaming platforms use it to identify your recording → Music supervisors use it to verify rights and process licences → Without it, you can't be tracked, paid, or placed professionally Getting an ISRC is free if you distribute through DistroKid, TuneCore, CD Baby, or any major distributor. They assign one automatically when you upload. If you've released music independently and never used a distributor, you can register directly through your national ISRC agency. In Nigeria: MCSN (Musical Copyright Society of Nigeria) can help. In South Africa: RISA (Recording Industry of South Africa). This is a 15-minute task. It unlocks you for the global rights system permanently. No ISRC = no placement. It's that simple. CTA: DM us "ISRC" and we'll send you a step-by-step setup guide.

Pre-cleared inventory They want to know, before they press play, that the rights are clean. Not "we'll sort it once you select the track." Pre-cleared. Documented. Ready to licence on confirmation. 2. Brief-matched curation Not a library. A human who understands the brief, matches it to real options, and sends five — not fifty. The volume problem is the problem they are most tired of. 3. A reliable African music pipeline The demand is explicit and growing. The commissioning of African original content is accelerating. The need for authentic African sound in global productions is not a trend — it is a structural shift. And there is no trusted pipeline delivering it at the standard production requires. These are not complicated asks. They are unsolved because the infrastructure has not been built. This is what SyncMaster was designed to address: pre-cleared composers, brief-matched curation, and a direct pipeline from African talent to global production.
