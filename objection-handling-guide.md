# SyncMaster Objection Handling Guide

Based on analysis of SyncMaster content, here are the top objections and response frameworks for both composers (Tunde) and producers (Amara).

## Composer Objections (Tunde)

### 1. "I don't understand rights - it's too complicated"
**Why they say it**: Rights terminology is confusing, fear of making mistakes that could block placements
**Response approach**: Acknowledge complexity, position SyncMaster as the guide that simplifies
**Proof point**: "We've helped 9 composers clear rights in Month 2 - none had prior sync experience"
**Follow-up question**: "What part of rights feels most confusing to you - ownership, samples, or publishing?"

### 2. "I don't have time to fix my metadata/stems"  
**Why they say it**: Perceived as admin work that takes away from creating music
**Response approach**: Frame as investment that unlocks paying opportunities
**Proof point**: "Composers who complete metadata/stems see 3x more brief matches"
**Follow-up question**: "If fixing your metadata led to a $10,000 placement, how much time would that be worth?"

### 3. "Why should I trust you with my music?"
**Why they sayit**: Fear of exploitation, losing rights, or getting nothing in return
**Response approach**: Emphasize vetting process, transparency, and composer control
**Proof point**: "All 9 vetted composers in Month 2 retained 100% of their rights and masters"
**Follow-up question**: "What would need to happen for you to feel confident sharing your music with us?"

### 4. "Is there really money in this for African composers?"
**Why they say it**: Skepticism based on historical exclusion from sync market
**Response approach**: Share specific placement numbers and composer success stories
**Proof point**: "Lagos composer earned more from one Netflix scene than 6 months of streaming"
**Follow-up question**: "Have you ever received a placement offer but didn't know how to respond?"

### 5. "I tried before and got nowhere"
**Why they say it**: Past frustration with lack of responses or dead ends
**Response approach**: Differentiate SyncMaster's structured approach from random submissions
**Proof point": "Average brief-to-delivery time is 31 hours in Month 2 (was 42 in Month 1)"
**Follow-up question**: "What happened the last time you submitted music for a sync opportunity?"

## Producer Objections (Amara)

### 1. "I don't have time to vet new sources"
**Why they say it**: Overwhelmed with submissions, wary of additional workflow
**Response approach**: Position as time-saver that reduces their workload
**Proof point": "Supervisors using SyncMaster spend 5 hours on rights confirmation (was 9 hours)"
**Follow-up question**: "How much time do you currently spend clearing rights per brief?"

### 2. "How do I know the rights are clean?"
**Why they say it**: Fear of legal issues, production delays, or having to replace music
**Response approach**: Show documentation process and verification steps
**Proof point": "0 rights issues in Month 2 placements - all documentation provided upfront"
**Follow-up question": "What would need to happen for you to trust a new source's rights claims?"

### 3. "What if the composer disappears after I pick a track?"
**Why they say it": Risk of production delays if composer becomes unresponsive
**Response approach": Highlight response time guarantees and system reliability
**Proof point": "Average composer response time is under 24 hours in our system"
**Follow-up question": "What's the longest you've waited for a composer to respond after shortlisting?"

### 4. "Is the quality actually there for my brief?"
**Why they say it": Concern that "African music" means only certain genres or quality levels
**Response approach": Demonstrate range and specificity of catalog matching
**Proof point": "We've matched briefs for orchestral Afrofusion, hopeful organic, and cinematic tense"
**Follow-up question": "What specific qualities are you looking for in your current brief?"

### 5. "I already have go-to sources - why switch?"
**Why they say it": Satisfaction with existing workflow, resistance to change
**Response approach": Show complementary value - fills gaps in current sources
**Proof point": "Supervisors in 3 new markets reached out to us in Month 2 via content, not outbound"
**Follow-up question": "What's one type of brief or music style that's hard to find in your current sources?"

## Response Framework Template

For each objection, use this structure:

1. **Validate**: "That's completely understandable - many [composers/producers] feel that way initially"
2. **Reframe**: "What we've found is that [different perspective or insight]"  
3. **Proof**: Share specific metric, story, or data point
4. **Question**: Invite engagement with open-ended question
5. **Next Step**: Suggest low-commitment action to build trust

## Content-Aligned Responses

Leverage existing content pillars for proof points:

- **Education Pillar**: Use when explaining rights, metadata, or process
- **Proof Pillar**: Use when sharing placement stories, vetting results, or response times  
- **Behind the Scenes Pillar**: Use when showing real workflows, timelines, or team actions
- **Culture Pillar**: Use when discussing African music quality, diversity, or opportunity
- **Industry Pillar**: Use when sharing market data, supervisor trends, or competitive insights

Example for "I don't have time to fix metadata":
- Validate: "Totally get it - you'd rather be composing than tagging files"
- Reframe: "What we've seen is that metadata isn't admin - it's what makes your music discoverable"
- Proof: "Composers with complete metadata get 3x more brief matches"
- Question: "What's one type of brief you wish you got more often?"
- Next Step: "Let's look at one track together - I'll show you exactly what tags to add in 5 minutes"

## Implementation Recommendations

1. Create quick-reference objection cards for outreach teams
2. Train on validating before correcting - build trust first
3. Use SyncMaster's own content as proof (placements, vetting stats, response times)
4. Align responses with the 5 content pillars for consistent messaging
5. Track which objections occur most frequently to refine approach over time
