# Content Pillars Guide

Five recurring content themes that make SyncMaster's feed coherent. Every post maps to a pillar. Brand voice: dark editorial — confident, not corporate. Mission: African music belongs on global screens.

Pillar Distribution — Month 1

Education 35%

Proof 25%

Behind the Scenes 20%

Culture 15%

Industry 5%

PILLAR 01

## Education

Tunde Informative · Accessible

35%

Demystify sync licensing for African composers who have never had access to this knowledge. Most of Tunde's peers don't know what a sync is, what a brief pays, or what rights clearance means. SyncMaster becomes the trusted educator before it becomes the platform. Every educational post builds goodwill, trust, and inbound applications. 

10 Post Topic Ideas

  1. 01What is sync licensing? — Plain-English explainer with real-world examples (that scene in a Netflix show, the ad you heard last week)
  2. 02What does a sync placement actually pay? — Breakdown of fee tiers: YouTube ad vs. Nollywood film vs. Netflix series vs. global ad campaign
  3. 03Master vs. sync licence — what's the difference? — Why you need both cleared before any placement happens
  4. 04What a music supervisor actually does all day — Humanise the buyer; show Tunde who he's writing for
  5. 05Why your SoundCloud link isn't enough — What you actually need to submit: stems, BPM, mood tags, ISRC, rights confirmation
  6. 06The brief breakdown — Anatomy of a real sync brief: mood, tempo, instrumentation, budget, exclusivity window
  7. 075 reasons African music gets rejected from global briefs — Not quality. Rights gaps, poor metadata, wrong format, no stems
  8. 08What is a one-stop clearance? — Why supervisors love it and how African composers can offer it
  9. 09ISRC codes explained — What they are, how to get one, why every track you release needs one
  10. 10How a placement becomes a credit, a cheque, and a career — The full journey from brief to payment to IMDb credit



Instagram Twitter · X LinkedIn

  * Nobody told you this in music school. Here's how sync licensing actually works.
  * A Lagos composer just earned more from one Netflix scene than from 6 months of streams. Here's why.
  * The brief said: 2 minutes, orchestral, hopeful, Afrofusion influence. Here's what that actually means.



  * Most African composers are one sync placement away from a career-defining moment. Most don't know how to get there. Thread 🧵
  * A sync fee isn't a stream. It's a negotiated licence. Here's what that means for your music.
  * The music supervisor didn't reject your track because it wasn't good. They rejected it because the rights weren't clear.



  * The global sync market is worth over $500M annually. African music has a fraction of the representation it deserves — not because of quality, but because of access. Here's what that access gap looks like in practice.
  * Music supervisors at Netflix, Amazon, and Ogilvy aren't looking for the best music. They're looking for the best music they can clear quickly. Understanding this changes everything.

PILLAR 02

## Proof

Both Aspirational · Credible

25%

Every placement, every onboarded composer, every brief received is a proof point. Use it. One real case study is worth more than 100 aspirational posts. This pillar converts sceptics on both sides — Tunde needs to believe placements are real; Amara needs to believe the composer quality is real. Proof posts are the highest-conversion content in the entire system. 

10 Post Topic Ideas

  1. 01SA composer Showmax placement story — Your existing proof point; told as a case study with composer background, brief, submission, placement, outcome
  2. 02Composer spotlight series — "Meet [Name]. Lagos-based. No representation. Just placed on [platform]." One per onboarded composer
  3. 03The brief to placement timeline — Real timeline from brief received to track selected to placement confirmed
  4. 04Rights-cleared in 48 hours — Document the curation turnaround to show producers what they're buying
  5. 05Before SyncMaster vs. after SyncMaster — Composer perspective on what changed for their career
  6. 06Producer brief results reveal — "We received a brief for an orchestral Afrofusion track. Here's what we sent back in 48 hours."
  7. 07Placement announcement post — Clean, confident, editorial format: Composer · Platform · Type of placement
  8. 08Composer application milestone — "20 applications. 6 vetted. Here's what we look for."
  9. 09The vetting process — What it actually takes to get onto SyncMaster's roster; shows Amara the quality bar
  10. 10First commission milestone — Founder post when the first fee is paid; human, honest, builds momentum

  * A Lagos composer just had music placed on a streaming platform with 50M+ subscribers. No label. No publisher. Just talent, documentation, and the right brief.
  * We sent 5 curated tracks to a producer. She picked one. The composer gets a sync credit and a fee. This is what the pipeline looks like.

PILLAR 03

## Culture

Both Proud · Editorial · Forward-looking

15%

SyncMaster has a point of view that no other sync platform has: African music is globally underrepresented, and that is a creative and commercial failure, not just a diversity issue. This pillar owns that conversation. It speaks to the cultural significance of what's being built — African sounds on global screens — without being preachy. It attracts composers who want to be part of a movement and supervisors who want to be ahead of the curve. 

10 Post Topic Ideas

  1. 01African music on global screens — a timeline — From Fela Kuti to Burna Boy to Afrobeats in Marvel trailers; show the trajectory
  2. 02Why the sync industry is sleeping on African instrumentation — Specific textures (talking drum, kora, thumb piano) that supervisors are actively searching for
  3. 03The Afrobeats crossover isn't new — it's been building for 30 years — Historical context; positions SyncMaster as riding a wave, not creating one
  4. 04What Lagos sounds like at 3am — Evocative, editorial content that makes the music feel cinematic
  5. 055 African film scores that should have won international awards — Cultural criticism; builds authority and community
  6. 06The genre labels don't fit — Why "Afrobeats" undersells the diversity of what African composers actually produce
  7. 07Afrofusion, Highlife, Amapiano, Genge, Bongo — and why global supervisors don't know the difference yet — Educational but cultural; positions SyncMaster as the guide
  8. 08African composers who broke through without a Western label — Celebration of self-determination; aspirational for Tunde
  9. 09What African music adds to a scene that Western scores can't — Creative argument for diversity of sound in film/TV
  10. 10The continent has 54 countries. The music industry treats it like one. — Hot take that sparks conversation and shares



Instagram Twitter · X LinkedIn

  * African music has been in global films for decades. African composers have been invisible for just as long. That ends now.
  * There are sounds coming out of Lagos right now that no Western composer can replicate. The world just doesn't know where to find them.
  * Afrobeats is a genre. African music is a continent of sound. Here's why that distinction matters.



  * The talking drum has been in film scores since the 70s. In 2024, African composers are still the last people in the room when those briefs are written.
  * Amapiano, Bongo Flava, Highlife, Genge. These are not subgenres of each other. Global supervisors are starting to understand this.
  * African music doesn't need a Western endorsement to be world-class. It needs a platform that already knows that.



  * The global appetite for African sound is real — driven by streaming, diaspora audiences, and a generation of supervisors who grew up on diverse media. The infrastructure to connect that appetite to the composers creating the music has not kept pace. That's the problem SyncMaster exists to solve.
  * Every major streaming platform has content set in Africa or featuring African stories. The music budget often doesn't reflect that. This is changing — and the composers who are ready will benefit most.

PILLAR 04

## Behind the Scenes

Both Personal · Honest · Direct

20%

SyncMaster is a founder-led business in its earliest stage. Building in public is a competitive advantage. Showing the process — vetting a composer, receiving a brief, matching a track — makes the platform feel real before it's fully built. It also builds the founder's personal brand, which matters more than the company brand at this stage for B2B trust. Authenticity is the differentiator. 

10 Post Topic Ideas

  1. 01Why I'm building SyncMaster — Founder origin story; the moment the gap became undeniable
  2. 02What a vetting session looks like — Real process: listening to a submission, checking rights docs, making the call
  3. 03The first brief we ever received — What it was, what we sent back, what happened
  4. 04Building a sync platform from Lagos with no external funding — Honest about the constraints; aspirational about the vision
  5. 05How we match a composer to a brief — The decision framework; what makes a track right for a brief
  6. 06Week 3 of building — what's working, what's not — Weekly transparency post; builds follow-through and accountability
  7. 07What we look for when vetting a composer — 5 criteria; shows Tunde what to aim for, shows Amara the quality bar
  8. 08The rejection email we had to write — Honest, empathetic; shows character and standards
  9. 09Our first composer onboarded — here's their story — Personal, specific, celebrates the milestone
  10. 10What the platform looks like right now vs. what it will be — Honest roadmap; builds excitement without overpromising



Instagram Twitter · X LinkedIn

  * Here's what happened the first time we received a real brief. We had 48 hours.
  * I'm building a sync platform for African composers. This is week 4. Here's what I've learned.
  * We rejected a submission last week. Here's exactly why — and what would have made it a yes.



  * Building a curated sync platform in Lagos. No playbook for this. Writing it as we go. Here's week 3.
  * We received our first producer brief this week. African music, 90 seconds, hopeful tone, one-stop clearance required. Here's how we matched it.
  * The vetting call took 45 minutes. The rejection email took 2 hours to write. Standards have a cost.



  * I'm building SyncMaster because I watched technically exceptional African composers get passed over for sync placements — not because their music wasn't good enough, but because no one had built the infrastructure to get it in front of the right people. This is week one of changing that.
  * Three things I've learned about building a music marketplace in the first 30 days: (1) trust is the product before the platform is, (2) curation is harder than technology, (3) composers don't need more exposure — they need representation.

PILLAR 05

## Industry

Amara Sharp · Authoritative · Analytical

5%

SyncMaster has an informed opinion on the sync licensing industry — not just as a platform, but as a voice. This pillar shares industry news, market data, and commentary that makes Amara want to follow because SyncMaster is giving her useful intelligence, not just selling to her. It also signals to Tunde that his work is entering a real, structured, global industry — not an informal network. 

10 Post Topic Ideas

  1. 01The sync market by the numbers — Global market size, growth rate, streaming's impact on sync fees; sourced and cited
  2. 02How streaming changed sync fees — SVOD vs. AVOD vs. theatrical; what a supervisor's budget looks like in 2024
  3. 03What music supervisors are actually searching for right now — Based on brief patterns; real intelligence
  4. 04The rise of non-Western music in global advertising — Market trend; Amapiano in UK ads, Afrobeats in US campaigns
  5. 05Why the old sync model is breaking — Libraries are too big, turnaround is too slow, rights are too messy; SyncMaster's positioning
  6. 06MIDEM, Sync Summit, Music Week — what actually matters at these events — Useful for Amara; positions founder as someone in the room
  7. 07The rights clearance crisis in global production — Why supervisors spend 40% of their time on admin that shouldn't exist
  8. 08Streaming platforms commissioning African content — what this means for sync — Netflix Africa, Prime Video Nigeria, Showmax; real briefs are coming
  9. 09The brief volume problem — Why music supervisors receive hundreds of submissions and select from five; the case for curation
  10. 10African music in global advertising — a case study roundup — Specific campaigns that used African sound; shows the commercial appetite



Instagram Twitter · X LinkedIn

  * The global sync market is worth $500M+. African composers earn a fraction of their share. Here's why that's changing.
  * Netflix is commissioning 40+ African originals this year. Every single one needs a score. The briefs are coming.
  * Music supervisors don't have a music problem. They have a discovery and clearance problem.



  * Streaming grew sync revenue by 35% in 5 years. African composers have not seen proportional benefit. That asymmetry is the entire business case for SyncMaster.
  * A music supervisor at a major streamer told me they spend more time on rights admin than on actual music selection. That's not a workflow problem. It's a market structure problem.
  * Afrobeats is in every major ad campaign. The composers getting paid for it are mostly not African. The catalogue is there. The infrastructure isn't.



  * Global streaming platforms are accelerating African content production. Netflix, Prime Video, and Apple TV+ have all announced significant slate expansions for African-produced content. The briefs that follow those productions will require African music — and the supervisors who close those deals will need a faster, cleaner way to find it.
  * The sync licensing industry has a rights clearance problem that costs production companies time and money on every single placement. The platforms that will win in the next 10 years are the ones that make clearance a feature, not an afterthought. This is what we built SyncMaster around.
